What a Participant Pathway to Housing Stability Should Look Like

A pathway should help a participant understand the process, receive appropriate support, and move toward a sustainable housing connection.

Last reviewed: July 2026

A general pathway

Programs and participants differ, and steps may overlap or repeat. This timeline is an educational model, not a universal mandate.

  1. Entry and orientation
  2. Eligibility and intake
  3. Needs assessment
  4. Individualized housing and service planning
  5. Referrals and service coordination
  6. Housing search or placement activity
  7. Regular reassessment
  8. Transition preparation
  9. Permanent housing connection
  10. Follow-up and stabilization

Participant Pathway Feasibility

HAA analytical framework

Participant Pathway Feasibility examines whether the administrative pathway presented to a participant was understandable, realistically usable, adequately supported, documented, and capable of leading toward housing stabilization.

This is an HAA framework for public education and records review. It is not an official HUD audit category, legal test, or compliance determination.

Designed for real use

A pathway can look orderly on paper yet remain difficult to use. Clear explanations, realistic deadlines, accessible communication, responsible handoffs, documented follow-up, and opportunities to correct misunderstandings can reduce administrative burden and improve continuity.

Reassessment matters because needs, program capacity, housing markets, and participant circumstances can change.
