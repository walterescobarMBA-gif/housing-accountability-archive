Records across a pathway

Intake records establish entry and basic information. Assessments identify needs. Service plans organize goals and responsibilities. Case notes, referrals, and follow-up records show activity over time. Accommodation determinations and notices communicate decisions. Transition planning connects one stage of assistance to the next.

Monitoring records, corrective-action records, and outcome reports help administrators and funders evaluate delivery, address weaknesses, and learn from results.

Five important distinctions

Authorized

Rules or funding permit the service.

Available

A program or provider has the service in principle.

Offered

The service is communicated or proposed to a participant.

Delivered

The participant actually receives the service or a completed connection.

Documented outcome

The record shows the result, disposition, or next step.

Administrative receipts

Public funding, administrative decisions, and participant services should leave documentary receipts showing what was authorized, what occurred, who was responsible, and what happened next.

These receipts make handoffs more reliable, decisions easier to explain, and monitoring more credible.

What missing records mean

The absence of one record does not automatically prove an event did not occur. A record may exist elsewhere, be retained under another system, be incomplete, or not have been required in the form expected by a reviewer.

Missing documentation can, however, prevent independent verification. A careful review identifies the expected record, searches appropriate sources, requests clarification, and states the limitation without overstating what it proves.
